WASTE MANAGEMENT PLAN

Scope and Purpose

The Pollution Prevention plan provides a guideline on how Offshore Global Logistics Ltd (Offshore) will assess its Pollution prevention plans. It therefore sets out the requirements and expectations in terms of pollution prevention. It covers Offshore’s operations in all phases. All potential pollutants that would rise from the supply chains require careful management to avoid negative impacts on human health, and environmental factors such as groundwater, soils, surface water and ecology. Offshore is required to ensure that all the requirements of the waste management are adopted within her own waste management plan. Key policies, Legislations and Standards All Offshore’s operations will be subjected to Offshore’s E&S policy and HSE framework. In line with Offshore’s E&S policy, all operations shall meet the following:
  • Host Country Environmental and Labour Legislation;
  • IFC Environmental and Social Performance Standards;
  • Sustainability principles advocated by the UN Global Compact;

Roles and Responsibilities

The Offshore E&S team has the responsibility of developing and implementing the operation specific Pollution Prevention Plan. This plan shall with complaint with the in-country legislation, the best international practices and in line with Offshore’s E&S policy and HSE. Offshore will ensure that her E&S supervisory appointments as well as any relevant appointments tasked with developing and implementing this plan are made upon proof of competency. The E&S roles and responsibilities for the Offshore team shall include:
  • Developing and implementing the Pollution Prevention Plan
  • Conducting training on waste management
  • Reporting all incidents related to waste or hazardous substances, investigating the root causes and coming up with mitigation measures.
  • Reviewing the effectiveness of the plan revising the plan according to changes in the legislations or other requirements emerging from international Best Practices
  • Working closely with the management to ensure the plan’s targets are achieved. Waste Management Hierarchy
Offshore operations will adopt a simple philosophy for waste management, placing a strong emphasis on the minimisation of waste, while adhering to the five principles on waste management listed below:
  • Reduce: If generated, reduce the quantity of waste.
  • Reuse: Where possible, use items for multiple use.
  • Recycle: Recycle if waste generation cannot be practicably reduced
  • Reclaim: Transform waste to a value-added product.
  • Dispose: If materials or energy are recovered from a waste it is classified as recovery rather than disposal.

Waste Management

Management Framework

Facilities that generate waste should characterize waste according to composition, source, types of wastes produced, generation rates, or according to local regulatory requirements. More specifically, waste management strategies should include:
  • Review of new waste sources during planning, siting and design activities;
  • Collection of data and information about waste streams;
  • Identify opportunities for waste reduction at source, as well as reuse and recycling; and
  • Define procedures and operational controls for onsite storage, treatment, and disposal.
When waste is generated at any stage of the operations, the following process will be implemented to proper manage it:

Waste Management Process

Waste Identification

Offshore will identify all the waste generated from her operations. The waste will be categorized either as recyclable, hazardous, and non-hazardous. Planning, Waste Prevention and Reduction Opportunities to prevent the creation of waste or the types of waste generated by business activities should be identified by Offshore. Processes should be designed and operated to prevent or minimize, the quantities of wastes generated. This could lead to a reduction in the amount of waste generated or the creation of non-hazardous waste instead of hazardous waste. Where possible, Offshore will implement the following for waste reduction and prevention:
  • Substituting inputs with less hazardous materials, or with those where processing generates lower waste volumes;
  • Undertake processes that create the least waste;
  • Instituting good housekeeping and operating practices;
  • Instituting procurement measures that recognize opportunities to return usable materials to the suppliers;
  • Exercise duty of care, particularly in verifying waste manifests and licenses for facilities that accept different types of waste; and
  • Minimizing hazardous waste generation by implementing stringent waste segregation to prevent contamination

Waste Handling

Non-hazardous and hazardous wastes will be handled to reduce the risk to workers. The use of Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) is critical in the handling of various waste types. In order to protect workers from the adverse impacts that could occur from contact with wastes, measures will be put in place to ensure that personnel handling wastes are trained in the use of PPE for handling waste.
PPE for Handling Non-Hazardous Waste
Key PPE required when handling non-hazardous waste will include the following:
  • Coveralls;
  • Eye protection (potential for potential of debris/dust getting into the eye of the waste handler)
  • Gloves;
  • Safety boots (chemical-resistant with steel toe and shank);
PPE for Handling Hazardous Waste
Key PPE required for handling hazardous waste will include the following:
  • Full-face or half-mask, air purifying respirators (IOSH approved) or face shield;
  • Coveralls;
  • Gloves (chemical resistant);
  • Safety boots (chemical-resistant with steel toe and shank).
Recycling and Reuse
Recycling plans have the potential to reduce the amount of waste generated. Offshore recycling plans will consider the following:
  • Evaluation of waste production processes to identify waste streams that could potentially be recycled;
  • Early segregation of wastes from recyclable wastes;
  • Investigation of external markets for recycling in the region;
  • Ensure that external manufacturers of technology acquired bear the responsibility of recycling (if applicable)
  • Establishing recycling objectives and track progress; and
  • Providing training to employees.

E-waste Management

Following must be taken into consideration when treating and handling electronic waste: PV technologies employ few toxic chemicals and those that are used, are used in very small quantities that they do not pose a material risk of toxicity to public health and safety. Furthermore, the EVA layers in PV panels keep broken panels intact; thus a damaged module does not generally create small pieces of debris; instead, it largely remains together as one piece. This means solar panels can be readily recycled. With regards batteries, the same supply chain solution as for solar panels should be negotiated. Failing this, old or redundant batteries are to be removed to an appropriate recycling facility.

Hazardous Waste Management

If hazardous wastes cannot be prevented, then management of hazardous wastes by individual businesses should be based on the following principles:
  • Hazardous wastes should always be segregated (separated) from non-hazardous wastes.
  • Understanding potential impacts and risks associated with hazardous waste as part of the operations;
  • Ensuring that the handling, treating, and disposing of hazardous waste are licensed and follows good international industry practice;
  • Ensure a registered waste contractor is in place to handle and dispose of hazardous wastes, as required; and
  • Ensure compliance with local legislation.

Waste Segregation

Offshore’s operations will identify, classify, separate and manage waste generated on site, based on each stage of the business operations. The identified wastes will be separated into solid and liquid wastes and further separated into hazardous and non-hazardous wastes based on the origin of the waste and its inclusion on hazardous waste lists, or based on its characteristics. Additionally non-hazardous waste will be segregated by type (plastics, glass, and metal, biodegradable). A critical component of the waste management process is the early identification and designation of the waste as hazardous or non-hazardous. The policy does not have a specific definition for non-hazardous waste but does have for hazardous waste. In this case the IFC Performance Standard definition for non-hazardous will be used. Non-hazardous waste will be physically segregated from hazardous wastes. Non-hazardous waste will be segregated by type (plastics, glass, and metal, biodegradable). The earlier waste can be segregated the better.

Waste Storage on Site

Facilities will be provided for temporary accumulation and consolidation of waste. Waste storage will be on a short-term basis. Some materials may be stored longer than others until volumes accrued are sufficient to support a selected waste management option. The E&S champion is encouraged to document waste storage within the operation for record keeping. This can be illustrated in a rough sketch or diagram of the operation noting key points that indicate the specific locations where the waste is stored. Wastes will be collected in bins, appropriately labelled and colour-coded for easy identification and evacuation (see Table below) Orientation labels such as ‘This End Up’ will be used for all containers bearing liquid wastes during all handling, storage, transportation, and disposal activities. The core characteristics of approved containers are as follows: Waste containers will be colour-coded and labelled for easy identification. Food and perishable wastes will be sealed in bin bags and containers covered at all times to reduce odour and restrict access by vermin and scavengers
  • Chemicals and any other hazardous liquids will be contained in compatible, appropriately sealed and labelled containers to prevent reaction with containers and spillage during handling.
  • Waste containers will be kept on pallets or other space buffers to prevent contamination in the event of a spill.
  • All waste containers will be maintained in good condition and stored with appropriate secondary containment
Minimal requirements for waste storage facilities are as follows:
  • Locate in safe areas with limited chance of exposure to hazards and accidents.
  • Facilities are to be enclosed with appropriate secondary containment to prevent spreading of waste and contamination of surrounding areas.
  • Facilities are to be lined to prevent contamination of soil and ground water.
  • Facilities are to be covered to prevent being washed off and spread by rain which could lead to contamination of soil, contamination of ground water and air pollution via wind-borne odours and dusts.
  • The entire area will be sealed off and operated with limited access.
  • Safety signs will be used to indicate potential hazards and restricted access.
  • Sufficient capacity will be maintained to contain anticipated types and volumes.
  • Effluent from the washing-down of concrete mixing and handling equipment will be contained within a bunded area of 110 percent capacity of the stored material. Effluent will be treated as hazardous waste.

Hazardous Waste Storage

Hazardous waste should be stored to prevent or control accidental releases to air, soil, and water resources in area location where contamination of non-hazardous waste with hazardous waste. The hazardous should be stored securely above the flood level and at least 20m from waterbody, watercourse, canal, or storage pond. If this is not possible, ensure secure measures are in place and storage facilities are lined to prevent potential leakages. Furthermore, they must be stored appropriately to reduce exposure to sunlight, wind and rain as well as minimise the potential for contamination of the environment. All hazardous waste (e.g. fuel, oil, used oil, paints, chemicals and contaminated soil) shall be stored in impervious containers in bunded areas of 110 percent capacity of the stored material to prevent contamination in case of accidental release. Waste chemicals will have SDS close by for easy identification and for emergencies. In addition, hazardous waste storage activities should also be subject to special management actions including behaviour of employees.

Transportation of Waste

All waste containers designated for off-site transportation should be secured and labelled with the contents and associated hazards, be properly loaded on the transport vehicles before leaving the site and be accompanied by appropriate documentation. Additionally, all transportation company vehicles should be thoroughly assessed to verify roadworthiness or are licensed to transport wastes.

Waste Treatment and Disposal

If waste materials are still generated after the implementation of feasible waste prevention, reduction, reuse, recovery and recycling measures, waste materials should be treated and disposed of and measures should ensure that there is minimal impact to human health and the environment. Management approaches should be in line with local legislation and could include the following strategies: On-site or off-site biological, chemical, or physical treatment of the waste material to render it non-hazardous prior to final disposal; and Treatment or disposal at permitted facilities specially designed to receive the waste. In addition to the recommendations for treatment and disposal applicable to general wastes, the following issues specific to hazardous wastes should be considered.

Training

Training needs for Offshore’s stakeholders shall be identified and a training plan will be developed. All training required shall be done before start of new operations. The key areas of the training to be done before commencement of operations include waste identification and segregation, handling of hazardous waste which shall cover storage, transportation, disposal, among others.
